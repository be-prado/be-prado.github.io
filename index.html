ABOUT ME

My name is Bernardo and I'm a third year graduate student at the University of Michigan pursuing a Masters degree in Electrical and Computer Engineering and a Masters degree in Applied and Interdisciplinary Mathematics. I started at Michigan as an Applied Mathematics PhD student and have since grown more interested in Machine Learning, Signal Processing, Game Development, and Computer Graphics. I decided to use my PhD credits to get two Masters degrees and learn more about the subjects that interest me. Feel free to look around for some of my highlighted projects.

I am passionate about all forms of story telling, I love video games, movies, books and music. I enjoy making music and I am interested in how Machine Learning and Signal Processing can be used to make audio products better. I'm passionate about learning and I'm always open to exploring a new subject or technique to better understand something! I think that's enough about me. Feel free to connect with me on LinkedIn if you want to chat!

  • SCALABLE AND EFFICIENT MACHINE LEARNING ALGORITHMS
    This repository features code that was written for a graduate course at the University of Michigan which focused on efficient and scalable optimization algorithms for machine learning. The problems solved include logistic regression, robust principal component analysis, matrix completion, dictionary learning and orthogonal dictionary learning.

  • PREDICTION OF BREAST CANCER SURVIVAL USING MACHINE LEARNING
    This project uses machine learning algorithms to predict the survival of patients 10 years after they are diagnosed with breast cancer. In addition, we sought to understand what are the most important parameters in the dataset for prediction of survival. The machine learning algorithms were implemented by ourselves and the methods used were random forests, L1 logistic regression and kernelized support vector machines. Our most effective methods achieved around 80% prediction accuracy.

ARTICLE SURVEYS

  • The Global Landscape of Neural Networks
    This article reviews some mathematical and computational results that seek to understand when neural networks converge to an optimal or nearly optimal output.

  • A Survey of Methods for Streaming Principal Component Analysis
    This article surveys different methods that address the computational and data-related challenges of principal component analysis (PCA). The issues highlighted are that PCA can be computationally expensive and that in practice, most of the data gathered is complete (e.g. has many missing entries). We review a series of methods that seek to address these issues.
